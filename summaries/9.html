Understanding Median Household Income Across U.S. CBSAs

Median household income is a crucial economic indicator that highlights financial well-being across different regions. This dataset provides insights into income distribution across various Core-Based Statistical Areas (CBSAs) in the United States, showcasing regional economic strengths and disparities.

High-Income and Low-Income Regions

At the top of the income spectrum is the San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara, CA Metro Area, boasting a median household income of $151,713. This region benefits from a high concentration of technology firms and skilled labor, resulting in significantly higher wages.

On the other end, Middlesborough, KY Micro Area records the lowest median income at $33,658. Many lower-income areas are characterized by smaller populations, limited economic diversification, and a higher dependency on industries that have declined over time.

Regional Economic Trends

Income levels tend to be higher in states with strong technology, finance, and government employment sectors. Areas such as Washington-Arlington-Alexandria, DC-VA-MD-WV Metro Area and Boston-Cambridge-Newton, MA-NH Metro Area reflect this trend with robust incomes.

Conversely, CBSAs in the Appalachian region, the Deep South, and parts of the Midwest tend to have lower median household incomes. States such as Mississippi, West Virginia, and Alabama feature several lower-income micro areas where economic challenges persist.

Key Observations

  • Technology and finance hubs command higher incomes: Regions like Silicon Valley and Washington, D.C. consistently outperform due to high-paying job markets.
  • Cost of living plays a role: Although metro areas report higher incomes, higher living costs often offset financial advantages.
  • Smaller metro areas with strong industries: Cities like Bloomington, IL, exhibit relatively high incomes due to stable industries and government employment.
  • Rural areas lag: Many micro areas still struggle with lower wages, fewer job opportunities, and slower economic growth.

Final Thoughts

This dataset underscores the varying economic landscapes across the U.S. While some regions thrive with high-wage industries, others face financial challenges due to historical and economic factors. Understanding these trends helps contextualize broader economic discussions and regional prosperity.
